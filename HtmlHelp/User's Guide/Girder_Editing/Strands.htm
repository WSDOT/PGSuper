Strands

On the Strands tab, describe the number and location of the prestressing strands.

Strand Details

The prestressing is described by the number of harped, straight, and temporary prestressing strands, and the jacking force imparted onto the strands. Locations of harped strands can be further described by adjusting the strand pattern at the end of the girder or at the harping point.

Entering Number of Strands

To enter the number of strands, simply use the Up/Down arrow buttons to dial-in the desired number of strands. The number of strands are limited to the number of potential strand locations defined for the Girder Library Entry you are using. The number of strands may be specified as the Number of Straight and Number of Harped (WSDOT method) or as the Total Number of Strands (TxDOT method).

Strands are filled in the order prescribed by the permanent strand fill order in the library entry.

Entering Jacking Force

You can explicitly enter a jacking force for each group (straight, harped, temporary) of the prestressing strands, or you can let PGSuper compute the maximum allowable jacking force based on LRFD 5.9.3. Explicitly entering the jacking force is useful when evaluating a design or existing structure.

To explicitly enter the jacking force:

  1. Remove the check from "Calculate Jacking Force"

  2. Enter the jacking force

To let PGSuper compute the maximum allowable jacking force

  1. Check the Calculate Jacking Force option.

Note : PGSuper will use the average jacking stress for all strands in the section when performing calculations.

Temporary Strands

Temporary strands can be pretensioned or post-tensioned. Pretensioned strands are built into the girder and are effective during lifting and shipping. Post-tensioned temporary strands can be applied to the girder either immediately before lifting, immediately after lifting, or immediately before shipping. Temporary strands are assumed to be removed after shipping. Temporary strands post-tensioned immediately before shipping are assumed to have no impact on camber and losses.

Use the drop down list to describe how temporary strands are used.

Vertical Location of Harped Strands

If allowed by the library entry, you can adjust the vertical location of the harped strands at the end of the girder and at the harping point to control stresses. For example: In the casting yard stage, if there is excessive compressive stress at the top of the girder, near the ends, the harped strands could be lowered to relieve the stress. Likewise, if there is excessive compressive stress at the bottom of the girder, between harping points, the harping points can be raised to relieve the stress. The location of the harped strands can be described by:

  • Distance between the CG of the Harped Strand Force and the Top of the Girder

  • Distance between the CG of the Harped Strand Force and the Bottom of the Girder

  • Distance between the Top-Most Harped Strand and the Top of the Girder

  • Distance between the Top-Most Harped Strand and the Bottom of the Girder

  • Distance between the Bottom-Most Harped Strand and the Bottom of the Girder

  • Eccentricity of the Harped Strand Group measured on the non-composite section

Note: Harped strands must lie within the vertical bounds defined by the girder.

Note: Hold the CTRL key down while changing the basis of measurement and the input value will be converted to the new basis.
