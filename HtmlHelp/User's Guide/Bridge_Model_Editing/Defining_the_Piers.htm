Framing

The Framing tab is used to frame the bridge starting with the lay out of the spans and piers. Each span and pier can be "drilled into" to further define connections, boundary conditions, and girder spacing.

In the grid, enter the pier station and orientation. The pier-to-pier span length is computed and shown in the Span rows.

Pier Layout

Piers are described by their station and orientation. The orientation can be a bearing (e.g. N 34 23 32.1 W), a skew angle (e.g. "-35", or "12 02 34.5 R"), or the word "Normal". Skew angles are defined as left (L) or right (R) looking up station. Positive skews are counterclockwise.

The buttons below the grid can be used to add spans, remove spans, and to layout the spans by span length.

The Edit Details buttons in the grid open a dialog for further editing the spans and piers.

Alignment Offset

The bridge reference line can be offset from the alignment. Typically the bridge reference line is the centerline of the bridge, but it can be any convenient line. The bridge reference line is a constant distance from and parallels the alignment.

Edit Pier Details

Pier details are grouped into three categories. The general layout of the pier is given on the General tab. This the same information that is entered into the framing grid. Options are provided for adjusting the bridge geometry when the station of a pier changes. The bridge can be simply moved or the length of the adjacent spans can be adjusted.

General

Connections

The connection geometry and boundary conditions on each side of the pier are described on the Connections tab. The Connection geometry is a connection object taken from the Connections library. It describes among other things, the location of the centerline bearing measured from the centerline of pier and the distance from the centerline of bearing to the end of the girder.

The connection boundary conditions describes the connectivity between spans.

The connection geometry and boundary conditions can be constrained to be the same on both sides of the pier or each side of the pier may have a different connection configuration.

NOTE: The connection boundary conditions are ignored if the analysis mode is set to Simple Span. See Chapter 7 for more details.

Girder Spacing

The number of girder, girder spacing, and transverse position of the girders are described on the Girder Spacing tab.

The input for the number of girders and girder spacing disabled when they are constant for the entire bridge. You can either go back to the General tab of the Bridge Description dialog and select the options to permit span-by-span definition or you can click on the hyperlinks adjacent to the controls. Likewise, you can change to defining these properties for the entire bridge by clicking on the hyperlinks.

Edit Span Details

General

Span details are grouped into three categories as well. The pier-to-pier span length is described on the General tab. When you change the span length, only the length of this span is changed. The stations of all piers after this span are adjusted to maintain the other span lengths.

Connections

Connection geometry and boundary conditions are described on the Connections tab. These boundary conditions are for the start and end of the span. If the boundary conditions are the same for both sides of a pier, changing them here will cause them to change on the other side of the pier as well. A message will be shown alerting you of changes that occur because of changes made here.

Girders

The number of girders, girder type, girder spacing, and transverse position of the girders are all defined on the Girders tab.

Girder names and spacing can be grouped as needed. In the example above there are three spacing groups; one between girders A and B, one between B and F, and one between F and G.

To create groupings:

1) Click on a column heading

2) Right click and choose the Expand command. This will expand a grouping such that there is a column for every member of the group.

3) Select a group of adjacent columns, right click, and choose the Join command. This will group the columns together.

As you can see from the example above, grouping is a good way to minimize input. Only three girder spacings needed to be input for the six actual spaces.
