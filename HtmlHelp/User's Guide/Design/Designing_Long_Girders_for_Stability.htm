Designing Long Girders for Stability

As girders become longer, lifting and hauling them becomes more and more challenging. In fact, instability from lifting and handling often becomes the controlling factor when designing beams over 100 feet in length.

To help designers better understand how the factors of safety against cracking, girder failure, and truck rollover are effected by the location of the lifting device and truck support locations, PGSuper plots the relationships in the Girder Stability View.

If the stability of the girder is controlling the design, and PGSuper's automated Girder Designer isn't zeroing in on an acceptable solution, you will have to adjust the design manually.

For the case of lifting the girder from the casting bed, the Factor of Safety Against Failure (FSf) is a function of the lifting locations. Temporary top strands have negligible effect on this value. The first thing to do is analyze the girder without temporary top strands, and adjust the lifting locations away from the ends until FSf exceeds the minimum value (1.5). The results of this analysis give you a lifting location. The required concrete release strength for the girder without temporary top strands can be back calculated from the maximum tensile or compressive stresses and the corresponding allowable stress. Of course, moving the lifting locations away from the ends increases the required release strength. Most large girders can be handled without temporary top strands, at the expense of higher release strengths. This is because compression in the bottom flange at the harp point usually governs. With smaller girders, this may not be true, since tension in the top becomes more of an issue.

The next step is to analyze the Factor of Safety Against Cracking (FScr) at shipping. For large girders, temporary top strands are normally required to bring FScr to 1.0. Temporary top strands have only an indirect affect on the Factor of Safety Against Rollover (FSr), which is a function of the load and roll stiffness of the truck. Temporary top strands allow the truck supports to be moved away from the ends while still maintaining FScr at 1.0 or better. Moving the supports away from the ends improves FSr. In any case, this analysis allows you to determine the truck support locations, the number of temporary top strands required for shipping, and the minimum 28 day concrete strength.

Since the temporary top strands will be needed for shipping, reanalyze the lifting configuration assuming the temporary top strands are tensioned prior to the release of prestress. This allows you to move the lifting locations closer to the ends while still maintaining adequate stability. Since the temporary top strands have almost no effect on FSf, moving the lifting locations closer to the ends will cause FSf to dip below 1.5. However, the temporary top strands significantly improve FScr. In this analysis, simply adjust the lifting locations towards the ends until FScr is at 1.5 or slightly above. The eccentricity of the temporary top strands, along with moving the lifting locations closer to the ends, allows you to reduce the required release strength.

The important thing to realize is that the primary variables in the calculations are the lift loop locations, the truck support locations, and the number of temporary top strands. Those are what you adjust to get the proper factors of safety. The concrete strength requirements are only output values, and are only a problem if they are outside the bounds that are defined as achievable.
